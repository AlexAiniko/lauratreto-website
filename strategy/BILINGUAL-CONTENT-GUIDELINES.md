## 1. LANGUAGE DECISION FRAMEWORK

Before filming or writing a caption, answer one question: **Who is this post FOR?**

### Decision Tree

```
Is this a movement demo with no talking?
  YES --> Dual-caption post (EN on screen, ES in caption or vice versa)
  NO  --> Continue below

Is this a Key West local post (restaurant, beach, event, community)?
  YES --> English primary. Spanish summary at the end of caption.
  NO  --> Continue below

Is this a personal/emotional story (Havana, family, identity, resilience)?
  YES --> Spanish primary. English summary at the end of caption.
  NO  --> Continue below

Is this a direct offer or CTA (assessment slots, program launch, booking)?
  YES --> English primary. Add one Spanish CTA line at the bottom.
  NO  --> Continue below

Default --> English primary with optional Spanish closing line.
```

### Language Split Target

| Language | Share of Posts | When |
|----------|--------------|------|
| English only | 50% | Key West local, CTA/Proof, most Movement Authority |
| Bilingual (dual caption) | 35% | Movement demos, exercise snacks, couple content, carousels |
| Spanish only | 15% | Personal stories, origin content, cultural identity, 1-2x/week max |

### Rules

1. **English is the default.** Primary audience is Key West locals (45+) and digital women (35-45), mostly English-dominant.
2. **Spanish is a superpower, not an obligation.** Use it when it adds authenticity, emotion, or reach. Never force it.
3. **Let the thought choose the language.** If it comes to Laura in Spanish, film it in Spanish. Translating a thought that arrived in Spanish into English first will always feel flat.
4. **Movement is universal.** Visual exercise content needs minimal language. Lean into dual captions for maximum reach.

## 2. DUAL-CAPTION FORMAT TEMPLATES

Every bilingual post follows one of three caption structures. Pick the one that fits the post type.

### Template A: English Lead, Spanish Footer

Best for: Movement Authority, Key West Local, CTA/Proof

```
[Full English caption -- hook, body, CTA]

--

[Spanish translation or summary, 2-3 sentences max]
[Spanish CTA if applicable]
```

**Example:**

```
Your hamstrings aren't tight. Your brain thinks they are.

Most people stretch their hamstrings every day and nothing changes.
That's because the "tightness" is usually neural tension, not
muscle shortness. Here's the test that tells you which one you have.

DM me MOVE for a full assessment.

--

Tus isquiotibiales no estan tensos. Tu cerebro cree que si.
Aqui te muestro como saber la diferencia.
Escribeme MOVE para una evaluacion completa.
```

### Template B: Spanish Lead, English Footer

Best for: Personal stories, Havana/Cuba content, emotional content, Trauma/Empowerment

```
[Full Spanish caption -- hook, body, CTA]

--

[English translation or summary, 2-3 sentences max]
[English CTA]
```

**Example:**

```
De La Habana a Key West. 90 millas. Toda una vida.

Sali de Cuba con un titulo de psicologia, un cuerpo entrenado
para el escenario, y ninguna certeza de lo que venia. Lo unico
que sabia era que el movimiento me habia salvado cada vez que
el mundo se derrumbo. Hoy, eso es lo que le doy a mis clientes.

Link en bio para tu evaluacion gratuita.

--

From Havana to Key West. 90 miles. A whole lifetime.
Movement saved me through every transition. Now I pass
that to my clients. Link in bio for your free assessment.
```

### Template C: Interleaved / Side-by-Side

Best for: Carousels, educational posts, exercise snacks with text overlays

```
[English line]
[Spanish equivalent]

[English line]
[Spanish equivalent]

[CTA in both languages]
```

**Example (carousel slide or text overlay):**

```
3 exercises every desk worker needs
3 ejercicios para quien trabaja en escritorio

1. Thoracic spine rotation / Rotacion de columna toracica
2. Hip CARs / Rotaciones articulares de cadera
3. Cervical retraction / Retraccion cervical

Save this. / Guarda esto.
```

### Formatting Rules

- Use `--` (double dash) to separate languages. Clean, visible, not intrusive.
- The primary language always comes first. The translation/summary goes below.
- Summaries are fine. You do not need to translate word-for-word. Capture the meaning.
- Keep the CTA in both languages when possible. "DM me MOVE" works in both.
- Never exceed the natural caption length for the platform. If the bilingual version gets too long, shorten the summary language to 1-2 sentences.

## 4. PLATFORM-SPECIFIC LANGUAGE RULES

### TikTok (Discovery Engine)

| Aspect | Guideline |
|--------|-----------|
| **Default language** | English |
| **Spanish content** | 1x/week. Personal stories, cultural content perform well. |
| **Captions on screen** | Always in the spoken language. CapCut auto-captions. |
| **Caption text** | Short. 2-3 lines max in primary language. No dual caption needed in the caption field (TikTok users don't read long captions). |
| **Code-switching** | Highly effective on TikTok. Mixing EN/ES mid-sentence feels natural and gets engagement from bilingual viewers. |
| **Hashtags** | 5 max. Match the spoken language of the video. |
| **Algorithm note** | TikTok's algorithm serves content by interest, not language. English content reaches more US users. Spanish content gets picked up by Latin American audiences (bonus reach). |

### Instagram (Community + Conversion)

| Aspect | Guideline |
|--------|-----------|
| **Default language** | English |
| **Spanish content** | 1-2x/week in Stories or Reels. Carousels can be interleaved bilingual. |
| **Captions** | Full dual-caption format (Template A or B). IG users read captions. |
| **Stories** | Can be fully Spanish with English text overlay, or vice versa. Low commitment format, good for testing. |
| **Carousels** | Interleaved format (Template C) works best. Each slide readable in both languages. |
| **Hashtags** | 5 max. For bilingual posts, mix 3 EN + 2 ES. |
| **Algorithm note** | IG prioritizes saves and shares. Bilingual carousels get saved by both English and Spanish speakers. Double the save rate. |

### Facebook (Community + 50+ Audience)

| Aspect | Guideline |
|--------|-----------|
| **Default language** | English |
| **Spanish content** | Rare. Key West FB audience is overwhelmingly English-speaking. |
| **Caption format** | English only for most posts. Add a Spanish line only for personal/cultural stories. |
| **FB Lives** | Primarily English. If someone asks a question in Spanish, answer in Spanish. Natural, not scripted. |
| **Hashtags** | 5 max. English set. Facebook hashtags matter less, but keep them consistent. |
| **Algorithm note** | FB rewards comments and shares. English posts generate more local engagement. Spanish posts can work for shareable emotional content (friends tag Spanish-speaking family). |

## 6. VOICE AND TONE ACROSS LANGUAGES

Laura's voice must feel the same in both languages: warm, confident, direct, knowledgeable. Not clinical. Not preachy. Not generic fitness influencer.

### English Voice

| Trait | How It Shows |
|-------|-------------|
| **Warm but direct** | "Here's what I see." Not "I just wanted to share..." |
| **Authoritative, not arrogant** | "I've coached 1,000+ performances." Not "I'm better than your trainer." |
| **Conversational** | Write like she talks. Short sentences. Real language. |
| **No jargon dumps** | Use one technical term, then explain it. "Thoracic mobility, that's your upper back's ability to rotate." |
| **Premium, not salesy** | "I have two assessment slots open." Not "LIMITED TIME OFFER!!!" |

### Spanish Voice

| Trait | How It Shows |
|-------|-------------|
| **Natural Cuban Spanish** | Use Laura's real speech patterns. Not textbook Castilian. Not Mexican slang. Her Spanish. |
| **Emotionally open** | Spanish allows more warmth. "Esto me cambio la vida" lands differently than "This changed my life." |
| **Direct, same as English** | Don't soften the message in Spanish. Same confidence, same authority. |
| **Tuteo (tu, not usted)** | Informal. Laura is talking to peers and community, not patients or clients in a formal setting. |
| **Cuban expressions are welcome** | "Mira," "oye," "dale" are natural. They add flavor. Don't strip them out. |

### Tone Consistency Checklist

Before posting, ask:

1. Would Laura actually say this out loud? (If not, rewrite it.)
2. Does it sound like the same person in both languages? (If the Spanish version is formal and the English is casual, fix the Spanish.)
3. Is there a credential or story woven in naturally? (Not forced, not absent.)
4. Does it end with a clear action? (DM, link, save, share, tag.)

## 7. PITFALLS TO AVOID

### The Seven Deadly Sins of Bilingual Content

**1. Machine-translation captions.**
Google Translate Spanish sounds robotic. "Entrena conmigo" is fine. "Permita que le ayude a mejorar su movilidad articular" is a machine talking. If Laura wouldn't say it at dinner, don't post it.

**2. Word-for-word translation.**
English and Spanish express ideas differently. "Your hips are lying to you" translates to something like "Tus caderas te estan mintiendo," and that works. But "Your body keeps the score" does not translate literally. Adapt the meaning, not the words.

**3. Spanglish overload.**
Natural code-switching is great: "Mira, this is what I mean." But full Spanglish sentences that belong to neither language confuse both audiences: "Necesitas to stretch your isquiotibiales before you haces exercise." No.

**4. Treating Spanish as an afterthought.**
A rushed, two-word Spanish summary at the bottom of every caption signals "I don't actually care about my Spanish-speaking audience." Either write a real summary (2-3 sentences) or skip it for that post.

**5. Different personality in each language.**
If the English caption is bold and confident, the Spanish version can't be timid and overly polite. Same Laura, same energy, both languages.

**6. Overcomplicating the format.**
Not every post needs to be bilingual. A Key West sunset photo with "This is my office now" does not need a Spanish translation. Use bilingual format when it adds value, not as a checkbox.

**7. Ignoring regional differences.**
Laura is Cuban. Her Spanish reflects that. Don't sanitize it into "neutral" Latin American Spanish. The specificity is the brand. "Asere" is Laura. "Compa" is not.
